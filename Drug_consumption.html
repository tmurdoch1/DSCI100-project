Introduction¶

Cocaine is an illicit drug that belongs to the class of stimulants and is primarily administered through nasal inhalation. It is produced with derivatives from the coca plant, which is native to South America. In 2020, the United Nations reported that there were 21 million users of cocaine globally. Many researchers have begun to investigate the underlying indicators that make humans more susceptible to consuming illegal substances and to developing addiction.

For this project, we are interested in evaluating how one's demographics may predict the usage of cocaine. We are using a data set which was obtained from 1885 participants. The researchers looked at personality measurement attributes as well as demographic information while collecting their disclosed usage of 18 substances. We will be using the attributes of gender, age, education, ethnicity and country to predict whether someone may be a user of cocaine.

Visualize the data¶

Since our data is standardized, the scatter plot and regular bar plot don't present variable distribution very well. Instead we use the function "fill" to equalize the height of bar plot so we can compare the proportion of class based on different predictors, which allows us to find which variables are useful to distinguish whether a person may become a drug user.

Methods¶

We will use gender, age, education and ethnicity to classify whether a person is likely to have used cocaine or not. Cocaine users are defined as having used cocaine within the last 10 years, whereas non-users are defined as not having used cocaine in the last 10 years or as never having used cocaine at all. Furthermore, though not yet done, the graphs will be edited to shows the character values (ie. country name or level of education) rather than the standardized values shown on the plot.

Expected outcomes and significance¶

It is expected that:

  • Men are more likely to use cocaine than women.
  • Cocaine would mostly be used by the age group of 18 to 25.
  • Lower education levels alongside increased exposure to addiction, monetary hardships, and social isolation may increase the likelihood that a person would use cocaine.
  • Cocaine use may vary by ethnicity, for example we observe higher consumption by African Americans compared to caucasian populations.

The findings might have a number of important impacts. For instance, they might assist in the creation of targeted interventions that cater to the particular requirements of a specific group. Given the observed link between education level and drug usage, vocational training and employment help may be a useful part of treatment plans for those with substance use disorders. College campuses may be the focus of initiatives to minimize young adults' cocaine usage, while neighborhoods with high Hispanic and African populations may be the target of initiatives.

The findings could also raise several questions for further research, such as:

  • What historical events have led to greater usage of cocaine in certain demographics?
  • Why does a higher degree of education make cocaine use less likely?
  • How can public health initiatives more effectively reach groups that are more likely to use cocaine?
  • Are there any additional racial or ethnic demographic characteristics that affect cocaine use?
